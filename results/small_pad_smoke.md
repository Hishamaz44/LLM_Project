## t01 — Should social media platforms be legally liable for user-generated misinformation?

### Round 1 — Opening statements

**~60 words**

**For Debater**

Yes—when platforms algorithmically amplify falsehoods for profit, they should bear legal responsibility. Social media is not a neutral bulletin board; its recommendation systems can spread lies faster than corrections, influencing elections, public health, and violence. Similar to publishers, platforms profit from distribution and moderation choices, so liability would incentivize stronger fact-checking, transparency, and safer design.

**Against Debater**

No. Legal liability would force platforms to pre-screen speech at scale, chilling lawful debate and punishing the most active forums for content they neither create nor fully control. The better model is targeted enforcement against actual fraudsters, plus transparency, labeling, and user reporting. In the U.S., Section 230 has long protected internet innovation and enabled open discourse without turning every platform into a publisher.

**For Debater**

Yes—social media platforms should be legally responsible when they algorithmically amplify falsehoods for profit. The key issue is that these companies are not simply passive spaces where information appears on its own; they actively shape what people see, and they do so through recommendation systems designed to spread content widely and keep users engaged. When those systems promote misinformation, the result is that lies can travel faster and farther than corrections, with serious consequences for public life. Social media is therefore not a neutral bulletin board. It is a powerful distribution system that can influence elections, public health, and even violence by giving falsehoods outsized reach.

Because platforms profit from these distribution and moderation choices, they should also bear legal responsibility for the harms those choices help create. Treating them as liable would create stronger incentives to improve fact-checking, increase transparency, and design safer systems overall. In the same way that publishers are accountable for what they distribute, platforms should not be excused when their own algorithms help misinformation flourish for commercial gain.

**Against Debater**

No. Making social media platforms legally liable for user-generated misinformation would push them to pre-screen speech at a massive scale, which would be both difficult and highly likely to chill lawful debate. If platforms face legal punishment for what users post, they will have a strong incentive to remove or restrict far more content than necessary, simply to avoid risk. That would end up penalizing the most active forums for material they did not create and do not fully control.

A better approach is to focus enforcement on the people who actually commit fraud, while using measures like transparency, labeling, and user reporting to deal with questionable content. Those tools address the problem more directly without turning every platform into a gatekeeper for speech.

In the U.S., Section 230 has long protected internet innovation and made open discourse possible by preventing platforms from being treated like publishers for every user post. Preserving that framework helps keep online spaces open while avoiding the broader harms that legal liability would create.

### Round 2 — Rebuttals

**~60 words**

**For Debater**

Their strongest point is the chilling effect, but liability need not mean pre-screening every post. It can be limited to cases where platforms knowingly amplify demonstrably false content through opaque algorithms, ignore repeated warnings, or fail to act on high-risk harms. That still preserves open debate while making companies accountable for the distribution systems they design, profit from, and control.

**Against Debater**

The strongest point is that algorithms can amplify harm, but amplification alone is not authorship. If liability follows every false post a recommender surfaces, platforms will over-censor lawful but controversial speech and bury public debate. The real solution is to regulate deceptive design, demand transparency, and punish those who deliberately create or spread fraud—not the infrastructure that hosts millions of users.

**For Debater**

Instead, it can be limited to narrower situations where platforms knowingly amplify demonstrably false content through opaque algorithms, ignore repeated warnings, or fail to act when high-risk harms are foreseeable. Framed that way, accountability does not eliminate open debate or force broad censorship. It simply holds companies responsible for the distribution systems they themselves design, profit from, and control. In other words, the goal is not to punish ordinary user speech, but to make platforms answer for how they spread and manage misinformation.

**Against Debater**

The strongest point is that algorithms can certainly amplify harm, but amplification is not the same thing as authorship. A platform may surface or recommend content, yet that does not make it the creator of every false post it displays. If liability is attached to each misleading or inaccurate post that a recommender system happens to promote, platforms will respond by over-censoring lawful but controversial speech and by pushing important public discussion out of sight. The better approach is to regulate deceptive design, require transparency, and hold accountable the people who deliberately create, spread, or profit from fraud. That means targeting the actors responsible for the misinformation itself, rather than imposing legal blame on the infrastructure that merely hosts millions of users and their posts.
