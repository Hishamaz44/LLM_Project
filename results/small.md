## t01 — Should social media platforms be legally liable for user-generated misinformation?

### Round 1 — Opening statements

**~60 words**

**For Debater**

Yes—when platforms algorithmically amplify falsehoods for profit, they should bear legal responsibility. Social media is not a neutral bulletin board; its recommendation systems can spread lies faster than corrections, influencing elections, public health, and violence. Similar to publishers, platforms profit from distribution and moderation choices, so liability would incentivize stronger fact-checking, transparency, and safer design.

**Against Debater**

No. Legal liability would force platforms to pre-screen speech at scale, chilling lawful debate and punishing the most active forums for content they neither create nor fully control. The better model is targeted enforcement against actual fraudsters, plus transparency, labeling, and user reporting. In the U.S., Section 230 has long protected internet innovation and enabled open discourse without turning every platform into a publisher.

**~120 words**

**For Debater**

Yes—social media platforms should be legally liable for user-generated misinformation when they algorithmically amplify, monetize, or negligently fail to address it. These companies are not neutral bulletin boards; they actively rank, recommend, and profit from content that drives engagement, including falsehoods. That makes them more like publishers or distributors than passive hosts. Concrete evidence shows the harm: misinformation about vaccines, elections, and disasters has fueled measurable real-world damage, from preventable illness to violence and democratic distrust. Current self-regulation has repeatedly failed because the financial incentive is to maximize attention, not accuracy. Legal liability would create a strong duty of care, forcing platforms to invest in moderation, transparency, and rapid correction. Without accountability, the public bears the costs while platforms keep the profits.

**Against Debater**

No—social media platforms should not be legally liable for user-generated misinformation. Legally treating platforms as publishers for billions of posts would punish them for content they neither create nor can realistically pre-screen. The result would be over-censorship: to avoid lawsuits, platforms would remove far more speech, including legitimate debate, satire, and journalism. Evidence from moderation systems already shows frequent false positives, and the volume problem is enormous—content is uploaded at a scale no human review system can reliably police in advance. The better approach is to hold actual speakers accountable, require transparency, improve algorithms, and empower users with labels, fact-checking, and reporting tools. Liability would not fix misinformation at the source; it would simply shift the burden to private gatekeepers and shrink lawful expression.

**~180 words**

**For Debater**

Yes—social media platforms should be legally liable for user-generated misinformation when they algorithmically amplify, monetize, or fail to act on clearly harmful falsehoods. These companies are not passive bulletin boards; they design recommendation systems that reward outrage, repetition, and virality. That means they help determine what millions of people see, believe, and share.

The harm is concrete. Misinformation has fueled vaccine hesitancy, election manipulation, and financial scams. During COVID-19, false claims spread rapidly online and contributed to real-world injury and death. In elections, false narratives can suppress turnout, erode trust, and destabilize democratic institutions. When a platform profits from engagement generated by these lies, it should not escape responsibility.

Legal liability would create proper incentives: invest in moderation, fact-checking, transparency, and faster removal of demonstrably false, high-risk content. We already hold industries accountable when their products cause predictable harm; platforms should be no different when their systems amplify dangerous deception at scale. Free speech does not require immunity for negligent design. Accountability is not censorship—it is basic consumer and civic protection.

**Against Debater**

No. Social media platforms should not be legally liable for user-generated misinformation, because that would punish the messenger for speech it did not create. Platforms host billions of posts daily; they are infrastructure for public communication, not publishers who vet every claim. Imposing liability would force them to over-censor to avoid lawsuits, deleting lawful opinions, satire, breaking news, and minority views along with falsehoods. The result would be less speech, not more truth.

There is also a practical problem: misinformation is often ambiguous, rapidly changing, or impossible to verify in real time. During COVID-19, many claims initially labeled false later turned out to be partially correct or at least plausible. A legal regime that punishes platforms for imperfect moderation would reward risk-avoidance and centralized gatekeeping.

Existing tools already address harm better: targeted enforcement against fraud, defamation, coordinated manipulation, and direct incitement; user reporting; fact-checking labels; algorithmic transparency; and digital literacy. The proper balance is to hold speakers accountable for their own statements, while requiring platforms to act reasonably, not to guarantee truth.

## t02 — Should wealthy nations be obligated to accept climate refugees?

**For Debater**

Yes. Wealthy nations should accept climate refugees because they created most of the emissions driving displacement and have the greatest capacity to respond. Climate change is already forcing people from homes through sea-level rise, drought, and extreme storms, and poorer countries are least able to absorb them. Fairness, historical responsibility, and basic human rights all require shared refuge, not abandonment.

**Against Debater**

No nation has an unlimited duty to absorb all people displaced by climate change; obligations must remain tied to capacity, consent, and law. Forced admissions would strain housing, schools, jobs, and social cohesion, while rewarding governments that underinvest in adaptation. The fair solution is funding resilience, disaster aid, and regional relocation—help people where they live, rather than imposing open-ended migration quotas.

**For Debater**

The number of displaced people will grow as climate impacts intensify, and orderly resettlement is safer and cheaper than crisis-driven migration. International data show most displaced people remain in neighboring countries, not rich ones, so the practical burden should not fall disproportionately on a few states. Moral concern is essential, but obligation to admit is too blunt and unpredictable a tool.

## t03 — Is it ethical to use CRISPR gene editing on human embryos to prevent inheritable diseases?

### Round 1 — Opening statements

**~60 words**

**For Debater**

Yes—when used to prevent serious inherited disease, CRISPR embryo editing is ethically justified. It can spare future children lifelong suffering from disorders like Huntington’s or Tay-Sachs and may eliminate a mutation from an entire family line. Compared with repeated IVF screening and selective embryo discard, precise editing can be the more compassionate, life-saving choice—provided safety, oversight, and informed consent are strict.

**Against Debater**

No. Editing human embryos is ethically reckless because any mistake is permanent, inherited by future generations, and cannot be consented to by the child affected. CRISPR still risks off-target mutations, mosaicism, and unintended developmental effects. We can prevent many inherited diseases with preimplantation testing, donor gametes, or somatic therapies, without altering the human germline and opening the door to heritable enhancement.

**~120 words**

**For Debater**

Yes—when carefully regulated, CRISPR editing of human embryos to prevent inheritable disease is ethical because it can eliminate suffering before it begins. Unlike somatic gene therapy, embryo editing can stop a mutation from being passed to future generations, offering a one-time intervention that may spare entire family lines from devastating disorders such as Huntington’s or Tay-Sachs. The moral principle of beneficence supports preventing predictable, severe harm when a safe, effective option exists. Evidence from gene-editing research shows the technique is becoming more precise, and stringent oversight can limit misuse. Refusing to use a tool that could prevent lifelong pain, disability, and premature death may itself be ethically questionable when the intention is therapeutic, not enhancement.

**Against Debater**

Using CRISPR on human embryos is not ethical because it crosses a line from treatment to irreversible alteration of future people who cannot consent. Real-world evidence shows gene editing is not yet reliably safe enough for clinical germline use; a mistake in an embryo cannot be corrected later like an ordinary medical error. It also opens the door to inequality and eugenics, where wealthier parents could buy genetic advantages and society starts deciding which traits are acceptable.
