## t01 — Should social media platforms be legally liable for user-generated misinformation?

### Round 1 — Opening statements

**~60 words**

**For Debater**

Social media platforms are not neutral; their algorithms amplify misinformation for profit, causing real harm. The COVID-19 infodemic proved deadly—falsehoods on Facebook alone led to vaccine hesitancy and preventable deaths. With great power comes responsibility. Legal liability would force platforms to prioritize public safety over engagement, just as car manufacturers must for defective vehicles.

**Against Debater**

Making platforms liable would dismantle the open internet. Section 230 fosters free discourse; without it, platforms must become omnipresent censors, over-policing speech to avoid lawsuits. This kills startups lacking resources and muzzles legitimate dissent. The real antidote to bad speech is more speech and media literacy, not making digital town squares legally accountable for billions of daily posts.

**For Debater**

Legal liability isn’t censorship—it’s a necessary incentive to design safer systems, just as we hold car manufacturers accountable for defects they could have prevented.

**Against Debater**

Arguing against legal liability for user-generated misinformation is essential to preserving free expression and innovation. Holding platforms legally responsible for every piece of third-party content would force them into a role of preemptive censors, inevitably silencing legitimate but controversial speech to avoid lawsuits. The sheer scale is unworkable: Facebook alone removes billions of fake accounts quarterly, yet harmful posts still slip through, proving that perfect moderation is a technical impossibility. This would crush startups that cannot afford massive legal and moderation teams, entrenching giants. The wiser path is reinforcing existing defamation laws for individuals who knowingly spread harmful falsehoods, while protecting the intermediary role that allows a diverse digital public square to exist.

Without the threat of liability, platforms have zero financial incentive to prioritize safety over engagement. Just as we hold car manufacturers liable for defective brakes, we must hold platforms accountable when their core product—the algorithmic amplification system—predictably causes mass harm. Liability shifts the immense cost of this damage back to the corporations profiting from it, forcing a fundamental redesign toward public safety.

**Against Debater**

Arguing against legal liability for platforms is a defence of free expression, innovation, and logical consistency. Holding platforms legally responsible for user-generated misinformation would create a disastrous paradox: to avoid lawsuits, they would have to become arbiters of truth, over-censoring content and stifling the very democratic discourse they are meant to protect. The inevitable result would be the suppression of controversial but legitimate speech, from political dissent to emerging science.

The practical impossibility is even more damning. With billions of daily posts, no AI or human moderation system can adjudicate complex, context-dependent truth claims in real time. The European Union’s own disinformation reports show that even well-resourced fact-checkers can only address a tiny fraction of content. This liability would not stop misinformation; it would simply bankrupt all but the largest tech giants, entrenching their monopoly power.

Finally, this approach dodges the root cause. Misinformation thrives because of human psychology and societal polarisation, not platform architecture. The law already holds speakers liable for defamation or fraud. Targeting the platform is like suing the library for a book with factual errors. We should demand better digital literacy, not shoot the messenger and destroy the public square in the process.

## t02 — Should wealthy nations be obligated to accept climate refugees?

### Round 1 — Opening statements

**~60 words**

**For Debater**

Wealthy nations bear historic responsibility for the emissions driving climate displacement—G20 countries produce 80% of greenhouse gases. Pacific islands like Tuvalu, which emit negligible carbon, now face submersion. Obligation follows causality: having profited from industrialisation that caused the crisis, they must provide refuge for those paying the price. Justice, not charity, demands it.

**Against Debater**

Wealthy nations have no binding legal obligation under the 1951 Refugee Convention, which defines refugees based on persecution, not environmental factors. Such a sweeping mandate distorts asylum systems and incentivizes mass migration over adaptive resilience. Resources are better spent helping nations mitigate climate impacts locally, stabilizing populations rather than enabling unsustainable, permanent displacement flows.

For low-lying nations like Tuvalu, rising seas are an existential threat, yet their citizens’ per-capita emissions are negligible. Having disproportionately caused the damage, wealthy nations have a moral and legal duty under the principle of “common but differentiated responsibilities” to accept those rendered stateless by their actions. It is not charity; it is accountability. ## t03 — Is it ethical to use CRISPR gene editing on human embryos to prevent inheritable diseases?

### Round 1 — Opening statements

**~60 words**

**For Debater**

Using CRISPR to edit human embryos can eradicate devastating inheritable diseases like Huntington’s or cystic fibrosis before birth. This isn’t eugenics—it’s compassionate medicine. Early research has corrected disease-causing mutations in viable human embryos with increasing precision. If we can safely spare a child a lifetime of suffering, we have a moral imperative to do so.

**Against Debater**

CRISPR on human embryos is unnecessary and unethical. Preimplantation genetic diagnosis already allows parents to select disease-free embryos without altering DNA. CRISPR causes irreversible off-target mutations—studies show large deletions and rearrangements. It permanently alters the human germline without consent, and opens the door to genetic enhancement, deepening social inequality and eugenic abuses.

**~120 words**

**For Debater**

Using CRISPR to edit human embryos is a profound moral imperative when it prevents devastating inheritable diseases like Tay-Sachs, Huntington’s, or cystic fibrosis. In 2017, scientists successfully edited a heart-disease mutation in human embryos with high precision, demonstrating feasibility. Unlike preimplantation genetic diagnosis, which discards affected embryos, gene editing offers a curative path, aligning with a pro-life ethic of healing. With rigorous oversight and advancing accuracy, the ethical balance tilts decisively: the compassion to spare future generations from relentless disease outweighs speculative risks. To withhold a proven, life-changing intervention when we can act is itself an ethical failure.

**Against Debater**

Second, it violates the future child’s autonomy by imposing irreversible genetic changes without consent. Third, it’s a gateway to designer babies; allowing therapeutic editing normalizes enhancement, letting the wealthy purchase superior traits, deepening social inequality.
