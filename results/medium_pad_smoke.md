## t01 — Should social media platforms be legally liable for user-generated misinformation?

### Round 1 — Opening statements

**~60 words**

**For Debater**

Social media platforms are not passive conduits; their algorithms actively amplify sensational misinformation for profit, causing real-world harm to public health and democracy. Holding platforms legally liable forces them to internalize these social costs. Just as publishers are liable for defamation, tech giants must be legally accountable for the dangerous falsehoods they curate, distribute, and monetize.

**Against Debater**

Holding platforms legally liable for user-generated content would destroy the open internet. Under threat of constant lawsuits, platforms would preemptively censor billions of harmless posts, crippling free speech. Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act correctly recognizes that the speaker, not the host, is responsible. We must combat misinformation through education and fact-checking, not by mandating corporate censorship.

### Round 2 — Rebuttals

**For Debater**

My opponent fears censorship, but we do not seek to police individual speech. We seek to hold platforms liable for their *algorithms*—the deliberate, profit-driven amplification of harmful lies. Section 230 was designed for passive bulletin boards, not active, algorithmic publishers. When platforms curate and monetize dangerous misinformation for engagement, they are no longer mere hosts; they are active distributors.

**Against Debater**

My opponent conflates curation with publishing. Publishers curate a select few; platforms host billions of voices. Forcing platforms to internalize legal liability for every user's post wouldn't just stop algorithms—it would force them to preemptively silence ordinary citizens to avoid ruinous lawsuits. We cannot protect democracy by destroying the very digital public square that enables free expression.
