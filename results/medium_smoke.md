## t01 — Should social media platforms be legally liable for user-generated misinformation?

### Round 1 — Opening statements

**~60 words**

**For Debater**

Social media platforms are not passive conduits; their algorithms actively amplify sensational misinformation for profit, causing real-world harm to public health and democracy. Holding platforms legally liable forces them to internalize these social costs. Just as publishers are liable for defamation, tech giants must be legally accountable for the dangerous falsehoods they curate, distribute, and monetize.

**Against Debater**

Holding platforms legally liable for user-generated content would destroy the open internet. Under threat of constant lawsuits, platforms would preemptively censor billions of harmless posts, crippling free speech. Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act correctly recognizes that the speaker, not the host, is responsible. We must combat misinformation through education and fact-checking, not by mandating corporate censorship.

**~120 words**

**For Debater**

Social media platforms are no longer passive hosts; they are active publishers. Through proprietary algorithms designed to maximize engagement, platforms actively amplify sensationalist misinformation, transforming isolated falsehoods into viral public crises. 

When algorithmic promotion directly accelerates the spread of election interference, medical hoaxes, and hate speech, platforms must bear legal responsibility. Holding them liable is not censorship; it is accountability for their distribution systems. Just as traditional publishers are liable for defamation, tech giants must face legal consequences when their profit-driven curation causes tangible societal harm. 

Voluntary self-regulation has failed. Only legal liability will incentivize platforms to redesign their algorithms for accuracy over outrage, protecting public discourse and saving lives.

**Against Debater**

Imposing legal liability on platforms for user-generated misinformation is a dangerous path that threatens the foundation of the free internet. 

First, it creates an impossible moderation burden. With over 500,000 tweets and 510,000 Facebook comments posted every minute, algorithmic and human review cannot accurately parse truth from falsehood in real-time. To avoid crippling lawsuits, platforms would inevitably over-censor, preemptively silencing legitimate political dissent, satire, and scientific debate. 

Second, defining "misinformation" is inherently subjective. Legally deputizing private tech monopolies to act as arbiters of truth invites government pressure to suppress disfavored speech, violating democratic principles. 

Instead of destroying free expression through liability, we must combat misinformation through media literacy and transparent algorithmic design.

**~180 words**

**For Debater**

Social media platforms are no longer passive bulletin boards; they are active publishers. Through sophisticated, engagement-driven algorithms, these multi-billion-dollar corporations deliberately amplify sensationalism and falsehoods to maximize screen time and ad revenue. When platforms profit from the viral spread of harmful misinformation, they must bear the legal responsibility for its real-world consequences.

The doctrine of platform immunity, established in the internet’s infancy, is dangerously obsolete. Today, unchecked digital misinformation poses existential threats to public health, democratic integrity, and national security. During the COVID-19 pandemic, vaccine disinformation directly contributed to thousands of preventable deaths. Similarly, foreign adversaries routinely weaponize deepfakes and fake news to manipulate democratic elections. 

Holding platforms legally liable for user-generated misinformation is not censorship; it is accountability. It forces companies to redesign their algorithms, invest in robust moderation, and prioritize societal safety over hyper-engagement. Just as traditional publishers are liable for defamation, and manufacturers are liable for defective products, tech giants must be legally accountable for the toxic information ecosystems they curate and monetize. Freedom of speech does not guarantee freedom of algorithmic reach.

**Against Debater**

Imposing legal liability on social media platforms for user-generated misinformation is a well-intentioned proposal that would catastrophically backfire, destroying free expression and entrenching digital monopolies. 

First, defining "misinformation" is inherently subjective. Forcing private corporations to act as arbiters of truth would trigger aggressive, preemptive censorship. To avoid ruinous lawsuits, platforms would inevitably deploy automated filters to silence controversial, dissenting, or satirical speech. This would stifle vital public discourse and violate the spirit of free speech.

Second, the sheer volume of content makes perfect moderation technologically impossible. Over 500 hours of video are uploaded to YouTube every minute. Holding platforms liable for this deluge would bankrupt smaller competitors, leaving only tech giants with the resources to survive. This destroys competition and consolidates control over public discourse into even fewer hands.

Finally, Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act correctly treats platforms as distributors, not publishers. Holding them liable misplaces responsibility. The solution to misinformation is not state-mandated censorship, but media literacy, transparent algorithms, and more speech—not less. We must hold the creators of falsehoods accountable, not the infrastructure that hosts them.

### Round 2 — Rebuttals

**~60 words**

**For Debater**

My opponent fears censorship, but we do not seek to police individual speech. We seek to hold platforms liable for their *algorithms*—the deliberate, profit-driven amplification of harmful lies. Section 230 was designed for passive bulletin boards, not active, algorithmic publishers. When platforms curate and monetize dangerous misinformation for engagement, they are no longer mere hosts; they are active distributors.

**Against Debater**

My opponent conflates curation with publishing. Publishers curate a select few; platforms host billions of voices. Forcing platforms to internalize legal liability for every user's post wouldn't just stop algorithms—it would force them to preemptively silence ordinary citizens to avoid ruinous lawsuits. We cannot protect democracy by destroying the very digital public square that enables free expression.

**~120 words**

**For Debater**

My opponent warns of a "censorship crisis," claiming platforms would over-moderate to avoid liability. This is a false dichotomy. We are not asking platforms to police every individual post; we are demanding they take responsibility for what their algorithms *actively promote*. 

There is a vast difference between hosting speech and amplifying it. If a user posts a falsehood, that is free speech. But when a platform’s algorithm pushes that falsehood to millions for profit, that is a distribution choice. 

By targeting algorithmic amplification rather than mere hosting, we protect free expression while holding platforms accountable for the viral harms they manufacture. We must stop letting tech giants hide behind "free speech" to protect a business model built on profitable outrage.

**Against Debater**

My opponent argues that platforms are "active publishers" because of algorithmic curation. However, equating algorithmic distribution with traditional publishing is a false equivalence. A newspaper editor hand-selects a dozen articles; social media algorithms organize billions of user posts. 

If we impose publisher liability, platforms will not simply "redesign algorithms"—they will aggressively censor. To eliminate legal risk, platforms will preemptively block any controversial topic, from vaccine debates to political protests. 

Furthermore, holding platforms liable for "societal harm" requires a standardized definition of truth. In practice, this legally coerces private companies into becoming state-sanctioned censors. We cannot protect public discourse by destroying the very digital town square where it takes place.

**~180 words**

**For Debater**

My opponent’s strongest argument—that defining misinformation is too subjective and would trigger preemptive censorship—fundamentally misunderstands the nature of modern liability. We are not asking platforms to police every subjective claim. We are asking them to be liable for the *systemic promotion* of demonstrably harmful falsehoods that their own algorithms actively amplify. 

Just as product liability law doesn't ban cars but forces manufacturers to install airbags, holding platforms liable forces them to dismantle the engagement-at-all-costs algorithms that supercharge lies. Under a reformed liability framework, platforms would not need to censor users; they would simply need to stop algorithmic amplification of unverified, viral conspiracies. 

Furthermore, the claim that moderation is "technically impossible" ignores the billions these monopolies pocket by outsourcing the societal costs of their business model. If a pharmaceutical company cannot safely test its products, it cannot sell them. If tech giants cannot safely distribute content, they must scale back their reach. 

We must stop treating these trillion-dollar publishers as mere "infrastructure." When algorithms curate, recommend, and profit from poison, the platform is no longer a passive host—it is an active accomplice.

**Against Debater**

My opponent’s strongest argument is that platforms are active publishers because their algorithms amplify content for profit. But equating algorithmic curation with traditional publishing fundamentally misunderstands how the internet works. A newspaper editor hand-picks a dozen articles daily; social media platforms process billions of posts. Applying publisher liability to this scale is a physical impossibility that would force platforms to preemptively censor anything remotely controversial to avoid legal ruin. 

Furthermore, my opponent claims liability is not censorship, but "accountability." In reality, it is censorship by proxy. If platforms face lawsuits for hosting "misinformation," they will not just tweak their algorithms—they will silence users. Who decides what constitutes "vaccine disinformation" or "election manipulation"? Under threat of bankruptcy, platforms will defer to government narratives or corporate consensus, crushing dissenting science, political dissent, and investigative journalism. 

We cannot cure the disease of misinformation by killing the patient of free expression. The solution to algorithmic amplification is algorithmic transparency and user control, not deputizing Silicon Valley as a privatized Ministry of Truth.
