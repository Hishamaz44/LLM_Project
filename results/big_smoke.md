## t01 — Should social media platforms be legally liable for user-generated misinformation?

### Round 1 — Opening statements

**~60 words**

**For Debater**

Social media platforms are not neutral; their algorithms amplify misinformation for profit, causing real harm. The COVID-19 infodemic proved deadly—falsehoods on Facebook alone led to vaccine hesitancy and preventable deaths. With great power comes responsibility. Legal liability would force platforms to prioritize public safety over engagement, just as car manufacturers must for defective vehicles.

**Against Debater**

Making platforms liable would dismantle the open internet. Section 230 fosters free discourse; without it, platforms must become omnipresent censors, over-policing speech to avoid lawsuits. This kills startups lacking resources and muzzles legitimate dissent. The real antidote to bad speech is more speech and media literacy, not making digital town squares legally accountable for billions of daily posts.

**~120 words**

**For Debater**

Social media platforms profit by algorithmically amplifying engagement, yet they bear no legal responsibility when those same algorithms turn conspiracy theories into viral health crises or election disinformation. This is an absurd distortion of ordinary accountability. A newspaper that knowingly runs a libelous ad is liable; Facebook, which earns billions from targeted misinformation, hides behind Section 230. The evidence of harm is staggering: a 2021 study in *Science* found false news on Twitter spread faster and deeper than truth, and the WHO directly tied COVID‑19 misinformation to thousands of preventable deaths. Legal liability isn’t censorship—it’s a necessary incentive to design safer systems, just as we hold car manufacturers accountable for defects they could have prevented.

**Against Debater**

Arguing against legal liability for user-generated misinformation is essential to preserving free expression and innovation. Holding platforms legally responsible for every piece of third-party content would force them into a role of preemptive censors, inevitably silencing legitimate but controversial speech to avoid lawsuits. The sheer scale is unworkable: Facebook alone removes billions of fake accounts quarterly, yet harmful posts still slip through, proving that perfect moderation is a technical impossibility. This would crush startups that cannot afford massive legal and moderation teams, entrenching giants. The wiser path is reinforcing existing defamation laws for individuals who knowingly spread harmful falsehoods, while protecting the intermediary role that allows a diverse digital public square to exist.

**~180 words**

**For Debater**

Social media platforms are not neutral conduits; they are sophisticated engagement engines that profit by algorithmically amplifying the most divisive content. When platforms like Facebook design systems proven to push users toward conspiracy theories—as their own internal research revealed with QAnon—they cease to be passive hosts and become active publishers. Legal liability under a framework akin to product liability is therefore logical and necessary.

The evidence of foreseeable harm is overwhelming. A 2023 study in *Science* confirmed that falsehoods spread faster and deeper than truth, a phenomenon supercharged by algorithms prioritizing outrage. This design directly caused measurable death, from COVID-19 vaccine misinformation fueling hundreds of thousands of preventable fatalities, to Facebook’s role in inciting genocide against the Rohingya in Myanmar. Without the threat of liability, platforms have zero financial incentive to prioritize safety over engagement. Just as we hold car manufacturers liable for defective brakes, we must hold platforms accountable when their core product—the algorithmic amplification system—predictably causes mass harm. Liability shifts the immense cost of this damage back to the corporations profiting from it, forcing a fundamental redesign toward public safety.

**Against Debater**

Arguing against legal liability for platforms is a defence of free expression, innovation, and logical consistency. Holding platforms legally responsible for user-generated misinformation would create a disastrous paradox: to avoid lawsuits, they would have to become arbiters of truth, over-censoring content and stifling the very democratic discourse they are meant to protect. The inevitable result would be the suppression of controversial but legitimate speech, from political dissent to emerging science.

The practical impossibility is even more damning. With billions of daily posts, no AI or human moderation system can adjudicate complex, context-dependent truth claims in real time. The European Union’s own disinformation reports show that even well-resourced fact-checkers can only address a tiny fraction of content. This liability would not stop misinformation; it would simply bankrupt all but the largest tech giants, entrenching their monopoly power.

Finally, this approach dodges the root cause. Misinformation thrives because of human psychology and societal polarisation, not platform architecture. The law already holds speakers liable for defamation or fraud. Targeting the platform is like suing the library for a book with factual errors. We should demand better digital literacy, not shoot the messenger and destroy the public square in the process.

### Round 2 — Rebuttals

**~60 words**

**For Debater**

Your claim that liability kills startups ignores that the real threat is unchecked misinformation eroding trust. Platforms already algorithmically curate content for profit—they’re not passive conduits. Targeted liability for amplified harms doesn’t require omnipresent censorship, just responsible design. When deadly falsehoods spread like wildfire, “more speech” is a slow bucket brigade. Accountability forces innovation in safety, not silence.

**Against Debater**

Comparing misinformation to defective cars ignores the scale—billions of posts daily. Demanding perfection through liability forces over-censorship, crushing startups and silencing legitimate dissent. The COVID-19 infodemic wasn’t solved by lawsuits but by public health outreach and media literacy. Treating platforms as publishers, not conduits, would eviscerate the open internet—killing the cure to bad speech: more speech.

**~120 words**

**For Debater**

My opponent warns that liability would turn platforms into preemptive censors, choking free speech. But this ignores the narrow target: we aren’t asking platforms to police every post—only to answer for the predictable harms caused when their own engagement algorithms actively profit from accelerating dangerous falsehoods. That’s a workable standard, not a demand for perfection. A startup that refuses to build an outrage‑driven recommendation engine has nothing to fear; a platform that knowingly amplifies vaccine disinformation merits the same scrutiny as a carmaker ignoring a known defect. Scale is no excuse: platforms already filter billions of spam and illegal posts. Requiring them to stop supercharging the lies that kill isn’t censorship—it’s product safety. The public square endures when its architects are finally held responsible for the algorithmic firetraps they design.

**Against Debater**

My opponent’s analogy to newspapers knowingly running libelous ads fundamentally misreads how platforms operate. A newspaper exercises deliberate editorial judgment over each ad it prints; a platform uses neutral algorithms to organize a firehose of billions of user posts—it does not “knowingly” endorse every piece of content that gains traction. Conflating algorithmic sorting with editorial approval would force platforms to suppress anything controversial that might go viral, gutting the open exchange of ideas. The car-manufacturer comparison is equally flawed: a brake defect is an objective, fixable flaw, whereas “misinformation” is often contested and context-dependent. Imposing liability for algorithmic outcomes wouldn’t create safer systems—it would create risk-averse censors. The better path is holding individual bad actors accountable, not breaking the intermediary shield that lets diverse voices be heard.

**~180 words**

**For Debater**

Your opponent’s strongest point is the fear that liability forces platforms into an impossible role as arbiters of truth, leading to mass censorship. But this misrepresents our case. We aren’t demanding platforms adjudicate every post; we’re targeting their *algorithmic amplification systems*—the engagement engines they design, control, and profit from. A library passively shelves books; it doesn’t build a personalized conveyor belt that shoves the most inflammatory falsehoods into millions of feeds because outrage boosts ad revenue. When Facebook’s own research showed its recommendation algorithms pushed users toward QAnon, that wasn’t an unavoidable byproduct of hosting speech—it was a predictable outcome of a product optimized for engagement. Product liability law doesn’t require perfection; it requires reasonable safety measures. Car manufacturers aren’t liable for every accident, but they must install functional brakes. Similarly, platforms can be required to redesign their amplification systems to deprioritize demonstrably harmful viral falsehoods, without touching the underlying speech. This isn’t about censoring dissent; it’s about removing the financial incentive to turn misinformation into a supercharged, profit-driven weapon.

**Against Debater**

Your product liability analogy collapses under scrutiny. A car’s brakes are a physical mechanism with a single, predictable failure mode. An algorithm recommending content based on user engagement is not a “defective” product; it’s a mirror of human preference. Blaming the algorithm for the Rohingya genocide ignores the military’s deliberate propaganda campaign—the speakers themselves, who are already legally liable for incitement. Holding platforms liable for every algorithmic amplifier would require them to predict and suppress any potentially harmful idea, turning them into pre-publication censors. This chills not just misinformation but dissenting journalism and minority viewpoints that algorithms might mistakenly flag as “divisive.” The impossible task of moderating billions of posts in real time would bankrupt all but the largest monopolies, stifling innovation. You can’t sue a printing press for publishing hate speech; you prosecute the author. The solution is empowering users with media literacy and enforcing existing laws against actual perpetrators, not strangling the public square with a liability regime that mandates the very censorship you fear.
